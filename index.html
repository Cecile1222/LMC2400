Description & Objectives

  • Upon completion of the course, students will have developed the following skills:
  • A broad understanding of the historical development and cultural impact of various media.
  • A set of theoretical tools for analyzing media texts critically.
  • A diverse vocabulary of formal and cultural elements of screen-based media texts and other cultural artifacts.
  • The ability to communicate analysis clearly and concretely through both written and oral communication.
  • How to find and use scholarly research through GT library resources.
  • Respectful, thoughtful, and professional group discussion and collaboration.
  • Increase their awareness of the multiple ways that technologies impact, extend, and hinder information uses and behaviors based on race and culture.

Course Materials

  • All required readings will be provided via PDF on Canvas.
  • Videos, films, and podcasts may be assigned where appropriate.
  • Accommodations made for students who cannot purchase works.

Teaching Style & Expectations

This course is a seminar-style course, where discussion is the primary mode of instruction and learning. Respectful discussion and inquiry are at the heart of the academic enterprise. Meaningful and constructive dialogue requires mutual respect, willingness to listen, and tolerance for opposing points of view. Classroom and online discussions, like any academic discourse, should follow university norms of civility and effective communication.

In this class, Participation is mandatory because it will enable you to explore concepts within the class while also engaging in discussions and sharing ideas with your peers. Furthermore, I do not expect you to have any fully formed ideas or understand the nuances of the readings, but I do want you to ask questions, challenge ideas, and share relative examples!

Lastly, the assignments and activities for this class offer you an opportunity to apply the concepts and theories that you have learned, and I hope that you can apply them to the best of your ability.

Reading List

  • The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction (1935) - Walter Benjamin
    Keywords: Aura (Benjamin); Cult Value vs Exhibition Value (Benjamin) | Length: 26 pages
    Reading Guide Questions
    • According to Walter Benjamin, what fundamental element is missing from even the most perfect mechanical reproduction of a work of art?
    • Explain the distinction Benjamin makes between "cult value" and "exhibition value."
    • Benjamin draws an analogy between a surgeon and a cameraman. What is the basis of this comparison?
  • As We May Think (1945) - Vannevar Bush
    Keywords: The Memex (Bush); Associative Indexing (Bush) | Length: 14 pages
    Reading Guide Questions
    • What is the central problem that Bush's essay, "As We May Think," aims to address regarding scientific research?
    • Explain the concept of "associative indexing" and how it functions within the memex.
    • What is a "trail" in the context of the memex, and what new profession does Bush imagine will emerge from this concept?
  • Modernist Painting (1960) - Clement Greenberg
    Keywords: Modernist Painting (Greenberg); Flatness (Greenberg) | Length: 8 pages
    Reading Guide Questions
    • What is the core distinction between the criticism of the Enlightenment and the self-criticism of Modernism?
    • Explain the fundamental importance of "flatness" to Modernist painting's project of self-definition.
    • Does Greenberg view Modernism as a radical break from artistic tradition? Explain his position.
  • The Medium is the Message (1964) - Marshall McLuhan
    Keywords: The Medium is the Message (McLuhan); Extensions of Man (McLuhan) | Length: 11 pages
    Reading Guide Questions
    • Contrast the effects of "machine technology" and "automation technology" on human relationships.
    • What does McLuhan mean when he describes the "content" of a medium as being like "the juicy piece of meat carried by the burglar to distract the watchdog of the mind"?
    • According to the text, what is the "content" of any given medium?

    Keywords: Mummy Complex (Bazin); Objectivity in Time (Bazin) | Length: 8 pages
    Reading Guide Questions
    • What is the "mummy complex," and what role does André Bazin claim it played in the origins of the plastic arts?
    • Why does Bazin argue that the intervention of a human hand in painting "cast a shadow of doubt over the image," whereas photography's mechanical nature grants it a unique power?
    • How does Bazin define cinema in relation to photography and time?
  • The Myth of Total Cinema (1967) - André Bazin
    Keywords: Myth of Total Cinema (Bazin); Integral Realism (Bazin) | Length: 6 pages
    Reading Guide Questions
    • What is the author’s primary argument regarding the historical causality of cinema's invention?
    • What key technical elements were required for photographic cinema, and did their invention precede the idea of cinema?
    • What key technical elements were required for photographic cinema, and did their invention precede the idea of cinema?
  • The technology and the society (1974) - Raymond Williams
    Keywords: Mobile Privatization (Williams); Symptomatic Technology (Williams) | Length: 14 pages
    Reading Guide Questions
    • According to Raymond Williams, what is the central difficulty masked by common statements like "television has altered our world"?
    • The text argues that the development of broadcasting was driven by economic forces in capitalist democracies, rather than political ones. What was the major area of investment in early radio and television?
    • Why does Williams argue that the term "mass communication" is a misnomer for the way broadcasting developed in most capitalist societies?
  • Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema (1975) - Laura Mulvey
    Keywords: The Male Gaze (Mulvey); Visual Pleasure (Mulvey) | Length: 15 pages

    • How do the conditions of a cinema screening promote an "illusion of voyeuristic separation" for the spectator?
  • Rhetoric of the Image (1977) - Roland Barthes
    Keywords: Anchorage and Relay (Barthes); The Linguistic Message (Barthes) | Length: 12 pages
    Reading Guide Questions
    • What are the three distinct messages that Roland Barthes identifies within the Panzani advertisement, and what is the substance of each?
    • Why does Barthes argue that the photograph, in its literal state, can be considered a "message without a code"? How does this distinguish it from a drawing?
    • What is the paradox of the photograph's "real unreality"? Explain the new space-time category it introduces as the "having-been-there."

Attendance (15 Points)

We won't be taking attendance; instead, I will be calling on people in a semi-random fashion to answer questions.

Grading Policy: If your name is called and you are not present, the absence will result in a 5 point reduction.

To avoid a deduction:

  1. Notify me via email before class if you have a planned absence (illness, personal emergency, etc.).
  2. In the case of an unexpected emergency, notify me as soon as possible afterward.
  3. Documentation is requested for more than 2 absences.

Discussion Lead + Mini-Workshop (20 Points)

For this activity, you will lead the class session for one assigned reading. Your job is to help the class understand the reading and then experience one of its key ideas through a short, structured activity.

What you will do (in class)

You will run two parts:

Part A — Presentation (15 minutes, slides required)

Give a clear, focused overview of the reading and set up the discussion. Your presentation must include:

  • What is the central (research) question the author(s) address?
  • What is the main claim, and does it have significant limitations?
  • Who are the authors, and what enables them to make this claim?
  • What kind of evidence do the authors use to support their claim?
  • What is one strong example of this evidence, with direct reference to the text?
  • How does this reading connect to an artifact or observation that interests you?
  • What discussion questions would you like to pose to the class?
Part B — Mini-Workshop Activity (20 minutes)

Design a mini workshop that immerses classmates in an idea from the reading.

Your activity must:

  • Be doable in ~20 minutes (inside the classroom or immediately around it).
  • Be interactive (everyone participates in some way).
  • Make a concept from the reading felt / practiced / tested.
  • End with a brief debrief (2–3 minutes) connecting the activity back to the reading.

Activity formats can include (examples):

  • Quick drawing or mapping exercise
  • Whiteboard group modeling (concept map, flow chart, role map, timeline)
  • Short role-play / scenario-based exercise
  • Small “prototype” game (physical cards/dice or simple online)
  • “Observation task” outside the classroom + fast report-back
  • Structured debate with constraints drawn from the reading

Required: Prep Meeting (15 minutes, 1 week before)

You must schedule a 15-minute meeting with the instructor at least one week before your assigned date. In the meeting you should bring:

  • Your draft slide outline (or rough slide deck).
  • A one-paragraph plan for your mini-workshop (goal + steps + materials). we can brainstroming the activities as well!/li>

This meeting is part of your grade.

Deliverables & Deadline

  • Due (in class): The day your reading is assigned.
  • One week before (required): Complete your 15-minute prep meeting.
  • Bring/prepare: Slides + any materials needed for the activity (paper, markers, links, etc.).(Please let the instructor know if need any help with activity materials)

Rubric (20 Points Total)

  • 1) Required Prep Meeting (3 points)
    3: Meeting completed on time
    0: No meeting completed
  • 2) Presentation Quality (10 points) — 15 minutes
    9–10: Accurate, well-organized, shows strong understanding; highlights key concepts/themes; includes author context; asks strong discussion questions that deepen interpretation
    6–8: Mostly accurate and clear; covers major ideas but lacks depth, specificity, or strong discussion framing
    1–5: Confusing, overly summary-based, missing key themes/author context; questions don’t support discussion
    0: Missing
  • 3) Mini-Workshop Design & Facilitation (7 points) — 20 minutes
    6–7: Activity is tightly connected to the reading; realistic in 20 minutes; clear instructions; high participation; debrief clearly links experience back to concepts
    4–5: Activity connects to reading but is under-scaffolded, rushed, or debrief is weak
    1–3: Activity feels unrelated to the reading OR unclear instructions OR low participation OR doesn’t fit time limits
    0: Missing

Discussion Participation (5 Points)

Participation is mandatory because it will enable you to explore concepts within the class while also engaging in discussions and sharing ideas with your peers.

Rubric

  • 5 Points: Actively participate in discussions throughout the classes.
  • 3 Points: Sometimes participate in discussions.
  • 0 Points: Never participate in Discussions.

Assignments & Rubrics

Creative Reflection Presentation (15 Points)

Description: Students are required to conduct an in-depth analysis of their previous works (e.g., films, media art, performances) or a completed/ongoing design project by integrating their practical experiences with media theory.

Analysis Requirements:

  1. Explain your artistic or design body of work, including initial concepts and development process.
  2. Detail your conceptual framework, influences, and the reasoning behind creative or design decisions.
  3. Compare past insights with new perspectives gained through theory, research, or reflective practice.

Presentation Goals:

  1. Showcase the quality and originality of your work.
  2. Demonstrate the deep intellectual concepts underlying your creative or design decisions.
  3. Make clear connections between theory and practice.

Format:

  • Similar to an artist's pitch. Highlighting both creative and theoretical aspects.
  • Time Limit: 4 Minutes + 1 QA.
  • Due: Upload deck to Canvas by 8:00 PM the day before your presentation.

Rubric

  • 5 Points: Integrate practical experiences with media theory to analyze artistic body of work.
  • 5 Points: Compare past insights with new perspectives, showcasing evolution of understanding.
  • 5 Points: Structure presentation clearly to effectively communicate ideas and theoretical insights.
  • Late Policy: Deduction of 3 points for each day late. >3 days not accepted.

Decoding Art Dialogue Presentations (Video + Canvas Discussion) — 20 Points

In groups of 2–3, each student selects one artwork from a museum (online or in person) and creates a recorded video presentation analyzing the work through a media studies lens. Your group’s presentations must be designed in conversation—your separate videos should clearly contrast, respond to, or build on each other’s ideas.

Requirements

A. Artwork + Analysis (individual)

Select one artwork and address following:

  • Media + Medium: Material/technical form and how it shapes perception.
  • Style + Historical Context
  • Artist Background
  • Theoretical Analysis: Course concepts/readings.
  • Speculation: How it could inspire new directions in media practice.
B. “Dialogue” requirement (group)

Your group must demonstrate an intentional dialogue across videos. Each student must include at least one of the following (recommended: do both):

  • A clear section in the video: “In conversation with my group…”
  • 1 slide (shown in video) that directly references a group member’s artwork and draws a contrast or connection using a course concept.

Deliverables

Deliverable 1 — Group Canvas Discussion Post (with videos)

One Canvas Discussion post per group that includes:

  • Group name + members
  • Artwork info for each student (artist, title, year, museum)
  • Each student’s video presentation embedded/linked/uploaded in the post
  • Still image(s) of each artwork + citation/credit

Due: March 16 (Canvas Discussion Post + videos).

Deliverable 2 — Peer comments (individual)

Each student must watch presentations and leave comments on at least 3 other groups’ posts. Each comment must include:

  • A connection to one course concept
  • One question
  • One extension (example, counterpoint, or connection to another reading/artwork)

Due: March 25 (Comments).

Deliverable 3 — Group reflection report (500 words)

Each group submits one report (~500 words) answering:

  • What you learned from other groups’ presentations (be specific)
  • Concept reflection: how this experience changed your understanding of one course concept (define it briefly + explain the shift)

Due: March 25 (Report).

Time + Format Guidelines (for videos)

  • Each student records 5–7 minutes (recommended)
  • Show visuals of the artwork (and any supporting images)
  • Audio must be clear; your face on camera is optional unless specified otherwise

Rubric (20 Points Total)

Baseline completion (12 points total)
  • 6 pts: Video completed and posted in the group Canvas Discussion post by March 16
  • 3 pts: Peer comments completed (3 comments minimum) by March 25
  • 3 pts: Group report completed (500 words) by March 25
Plus points for quality (up to +8)
  • +3 — Analysis depth + accuracy: Insightful media studies reading; goes beyond description; uses evidence from the artwork
  • +2 — Course concepts + sources: Concepts applied correctly; uses at least 1 relevant reading/idea from class effectively
  • +2 — Dialogue / contrast quality: Clear engagement with group members’ works; meaningful comparison rather than surface-level similarity
  • +1 — Clarity + structure: Well-organized, engaging, easy to follow, good pacing/visual support

Late policy: Deduct 3 points per day late. More than 3 days late: not accepted.

Final Paper: Speculative Media (25 Points)

Students will work in groups of 4–6 to conduct a speculative media analysis by selecting one existing media + technology artifact (platform, device, tool, algorithmic feature, format, workflow system, etc.).

You will propose a specific intervention—a function/workflow/aesthetic or interface change to add, remove, redesign, or repurpose—and speculate how that change could reshape media practice, culture, and power over the next 1–20 years.

Artifact Constraint: Your artifact must be an existing, identifiable system (platform feature, tool, device, workflow, algorithm, format). Broad topics like “AI” or “social media” are not allowed.

Intervention Spec

Define your intervention in 5 bullets:

  • What is changed (feature/workflow/UI rule)?
  • Who triggers it (user/creator/platform/AI/moderator)?
  • What data/signals does it use?
  • What output/action does it produce?
  • What is the intended value, and what could go wrong?

Analysis Framework

Apply these lenses to both (A) the artifact as it exists now and (B) your proposed intervention:

  1. Technical Aspect (Form): design, affordances, interactions, interface logic
  2. Organizational (Creators / Institutions): values, incentives, governance, labor impacts
  3. Cultural: cultural context, embedded values, intended users/exclusion
